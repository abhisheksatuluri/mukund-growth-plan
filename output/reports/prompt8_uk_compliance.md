# Prompt 8 Output — UK Compliance for Mukund's Coaching Business (2026)

> Source: ChatGPT Deep Research, delivered 2026-04-26 by user. Anchored in CAP Code, ASA rulings, GB Nutrition and Health Claims Register, UK GDPR, PECR, ICO guidance, HCPC, BDA, FSA, MHRA. Several ICO pages are under review post-Data (Use and Access) Act 2025.

## Strategic snapshot

> The biggest legal risks for a UK fitness coach in 2026 aren't the training content — they're **the claims wrapped around it**: precise fat-loss promises, dramatic before/after imagery, disease-treatment implications, scraping public data for cold DMs, drifting into regulated/medical territory.

Safest posture: market **process, support, habit-building** — NOT guaranteed physiological outcomes.

**Jurisdiction split:** GB (England/Scotland/Wales) uses GB Nutrition & Health Claims Register; Northern Ireland still has parts of EU food law via Windsor Framework. Matters if selling supplements UK-wide.

## 10 compliance rules ranked by relevance

| # | Rule | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No objective transformation claims without robust evidence; testimonials alone are NOT substantiation | CAP §3 / weight-control guidance |
| 2 | No specific weight/fat loss amounts/rates; no spot reduction | CAP rule 13.9 |
| 3 | Before/after = claims, not decoration. Need permission + proof of genuineness + non-exaggeration | ASA before/after photos guidance |
| 4 | Foods/supplements: only authorised nutrition/health claims (GB register), close to approved wording | CAP rule 15.1.1 |
| 5 | NEVER imply foods/supplements prevent/treat/cure disease — incl. inflammation, anxiety, menopause, GLP-1 equivalence | CAP rule 15.6.2 |
| 6 | Testimonials need permission + proof + relevance + context; incentivised reviews must be disclosed | CAP testimonials guidance |
| 7 | Disclaimers must be prominent + adjacent to the claim. Footer-only fine print is high risk | CAP rule 3.9 |
| 8 | IG DMs to public commenters ≠ loophole. PECR treats them as electronic mail marketing | ICO direct marketing guidance |
| 9 | Health/injury/measurement data = special-category. Article 9 condition required (usually explicit consent) | UK GDPR Art 9 |
| 10 | "Physiotherapist" + "dietitian" are protected titles. Don't represent regulated-practice wording | HCPC + BDA |

## Wording to avoid (specific phrases)

| Category | DON'T say |
|---|---|
| Exact outcomes | "Lose 10 kg in 8 weeks", "Drop 5% body fat in 14 days", "Get abs in 30 days" |
| Spot reduction | "Melt belly fat", "Target stubborn thigh fat", "Burn arm fat fast" |
| Certainty | "Guaranteed body transformation", "You cannot fail", "No willpower needed" |
| Vague wellness (supplements) | "Healthy", "Good for you", "Superfood", "Detox", "Wholesome", "Well-being" — without authorised claim adjacent |
| Disease/medical claims (food/supps) | "Reduces inflammation", "Fixes hormones", "Supports menopause", "Helps anxiety", "Stops pain", "Treats insulin resistance", "Supports PCOS", "Helps IBS", "Anti-bacterial" |
| GLP-1 / appetite suppression | "Natural Ozempic", "GLP-1 booster", "No more hunger", "Reduces cravings", "Balanced blood sugar" |
| Health-pro endorsement | "Doctor approved", "GP recommended", "Physio endorsed", "Dietitian backed" |
| Regulated-practice wording | "Physiotherapy rehab", "Clinical treatment plan", "Diagnosis-led programme", "Dietetic treatment", "Medical nutrition therapy" |

## Safer wording library (copy-paste ready)

### Body transformation / service copy
> "Online coaching for adults who want structure with training, nutrition habits and accountability. Designed to help clients build consistency, improve strength and support body-composition goals over time. Coaching includes programme design, habit support, check-ins and feedback. Individual outcomes vary depending on baseline, adherence, recovery, nutrition and other personal factors."

### Before/after framing
> "Client example shared with permission. Time period: 16 weeks. Support used: training plan, calorie-awareness coaching, sleep and step targets. This is one client's experience and not a promise of typical results."

### Testimonial framing
> "Used with the client's permission. Unpaid client review. [Or: 'Gifted review' if there was any incentive.] Quoted from their own experience; any factual claims have been checked."

### Supplement copy (compliant pattern)
> "This product is a source of [nutrient]. [Nutrient] contributes to [authorised function claim]."

### Social-media lead-gen (solicited, PECR-compliant)
> "Comment 'COACH' if you want the details, and we'll send the information you requested."

NOT this: scraping public commenters/followers and DM'ing cold about coaching.

### Scope-of-practice copy
> "General exercise and lifestyle coaching for healthy adults. Not physiotherapy, dietetic treatment or medical advice. If you have pain, injury, a diagnosed condition, or suspect disordered eating, we may ask you to get clearance from or work alongside your GP or an appropriate registered professional."

## ⭐ Landing page disclaimer template (use verbatim)

>
> This coaching service provides general fitness, exercise, nutrition-habit and accountability support for adults. It is not medical advice, physiotherapy, psychotherapy, or dietetic treatment for medical conditions. We do not diagnose, treat or cure disease, injury, pain conditions, hormonal disorders, eating disorders or mental-health conditions.
>
> Client stories, testimonials and transformation images are examples of individual experiences shared with permission. **Results vary** and depend on factors such as starting point, adherence, recovery, training history, nutrition, sleep, stress and personal circumstances.
>
> If you have an injury, medical condition, are pregnant or recently postpartum, take medication, have a history of disordered eating, or are unsure whether coaching is suitable for you, please seek advice from your GP and/or an appropriately registered healthcare professional before starting.
>
> Any nutrition or supplement references on this site relate only to claims that are legally permitted and, where applicable, to authorised nutrition or health claims. Supplements are optional and are not a substitute for a varied, balanced diet and healthy lifestyle.

## ⭐ Application form GDPR wording (use verbatim)

> I have read the Privacy Notice and understand how **[Brand name]** will use my personal data to assess my enquiry/application, contact me about the coaching service I requested, and keep my application data for the period explained in the Privacy Notice.

## ⭐ Content risk checklist (10 items — use before publishing)

Copy this into a Notion / Google Doc as a pre-publish gate:

1. ☐ Can I evidence every objective claim now, before publishing, with something stronger than testimonials?
2. ☐ Am I stating or implying a precise amount/rate of weight or fat loss, or spot reduction from a body part?
3. ☐ If I use before/after content: do I have written permission, proof it's genuine, and confidence that it does not exaggerate likely results?
4. ☐ If the post mentions food or supplements: is every nutrition/health claim on the GB register, tied to the nutrient, and worded no more strongly than the authorised claim?
5. ☐ Could any wording stray into disease, symptom or medical territory — including pain, inflammation, menopause symptoms, anxiety, hormones, blood sugar, appetite suppression or GLP-1 comparisons?
6. ☐ Are testimonials genuine, permissioned, relevant to the actual service/product, and clearly disclosed if incentivised or commercially connected?
7. ☐ Are disclaimers and limitations next to the claim they qualify, rather than buried in the footer or contradicted by the headline?
8. ☐ If I am using IG comments, DMs or public-profile data: is the outreach solicited or otherwise PECR-compliant, and have I told people what data I hold and why?
9. ☐ If I hold application data, especially health data: do I have a lawful basis, an Article 9 condition, a retention rule, a rights-response workflow, and processor contracts with the tools I use?
10. ☐ Does the content stay within coaching scope, avoid protected titles, and refer out red flags rather than coaching through them?

## Data protection structure (for the application form)

Three layers — keep separate:

1. **Lead-suitability data** (name, email, goal, etc.) → contract / pre-contract steps or legitimate interests
2. **Health data** (injuries, conditions, medications, body comp) → Article 9 explicit consent (special-category)
3. **Ongoing marketing consent** (email / SMS / DM) → PECR/GDPR analysis, NOT bundled into application

### Required vendor processor contracts
Any tool that holds applications or lead notes (Tally / Typeform / Cal.com / Calendly / Mailchimp / ActiveCampaign / Stripe / GoHighLevel / Notion / Airtable / Stan / Kajabi etc.) needs an Article 28-compliant data processing agreement.

### Data subject rights workflow
Be ready for: access requests, rectification, erasure, restriction, objection. ICO: respond within **1 calendar month**. Direct-marketing objection is **absolute**. Maintain suppression list.

## Medical/scope red flags — refer out, don't coach through

> **Red-flag symptoms requiring urgent medical referral** (per NHS guidance, not coaching territory):
> - New bladder or bowel symptoms
> - Saddle numbness
> - Severe or worsening leg weakness
> - Sudden loss of sexual function
> - Fever with back pain
> - Suspected eating disorder

Build referral language into onboarding screening and consultation script.
